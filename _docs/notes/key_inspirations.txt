# key_inspirations
Project : Split Screen Prototype
Created : 2026-03-23
====================

## Notes
--------

1. Kingdom (Raw Games / Noio)
The original structural inspiration. The core design (in my interpretation) is safe zone
expansion through careful resource timing and sequencing. The fundamental tension is between
safety and expansion. Growing defended space is satisfying but creates the dread of losing it
all. The game's day/night difficulty progression creates the motive for the player to choose
expansion over safety.
	- Teaches: Timing as tension.
	- Multiplayer: Co-op doubles the decision density per unit time without changing mechanics.
	Players can do more - but must coordinate in RL to be efficient.
	- Why it didn't spawn a genre: Devs may see the purity of design and mistake it for incompleteness.
	Just adding features / mechanics will probably break the simplicity and depth.

2. Dungeon of the Endless (Amplitude Studios)
The spatial inspiration. Rooms act as discrete units of risk in the mental model of the player.
Resource allocation is the primary tension and the procedural map provides a fresh problem to
solve each run.
	- Teaches: Space and resource as the core tension (timing is controlled entirely by the player)
	- Experts will open every room for maximum profit. Beginners will limit open rooms to reduce risk.
	- Lighting and unlighting allow management of avenues of threat within the mental space.

3. Age of Empires I & II (Ensemble Studios)
The village  as identity. Players don't just build functionally - their self is expressed in the build
as well. Their base becomes a self-portrait and a reflection of play style. Random maps provide a new
spatial problem to solve each 'run'.
	- Teaches: Base building as player expression, not just resource management.
	- Risk: Players could become siloed in their bases, reducing player interaction.

4. Cat Quest I, II & III (Gentlebros)
Cambat accessibility inspiration. very pickup-able and enjoyable for non-action game players. Combat
should not be the challenge - it should be approachable and expressive. The challenge is the long-term
build and strategic choices. Even Courtney likes CQ III so that's saying something.
	- Teaches: combat as reward and expression, not as a skill gate. (Please go away souls-likes)
	- As the village grows more powerful, the character becomes more powerful - players then feel
	the feedback loop.

5. Fable I, II & III (Lionhead Studios)
Inspiration for a reactive world and social texture. The world notices and comments on your choices.
NPC's create a type of social juice feedback reflecting the player's expression back to them - hopefully
making the player feel like their chocies mattered.
		- Teaches: NPC reactivity as an emotional core, not just flavour.
		- Village NPC's who comment on your build, style (visual and combat), and reputation.
		- A bit of juice from the balcksmith is far more effective than 100 passive NPC's.
		- This is a system that will have to be included later but may have a huge impact on game feel.
